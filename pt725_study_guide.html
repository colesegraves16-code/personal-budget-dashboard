Lower Extremity & Pelvis

Bones of the hip and leg, the gluteal and thigh muscles, ankle movers, and gait mechanics. Use this section to reinforce and connect concepts to clinical practice.

Bones & landmarks
The pelvis — your body's foundation Bone ▼
What it is: The ring of bone connecting your spine to your legs. Made of three fused bones on each side — the ilium, ischium, and pubis — which together form the os coxae (hip bone). The two hip bones join at the front (pubic symphysis) and attach to the sacrum at the back (sacroiliac joints).

Ilium The big flaring "wing" — the iliac crest is what you feel when you rest your hands on your hips. Key landmark for locating L4.
Ischium The bottom "sit bones." The ischial tuberosity is where the hamstrings attach.
Pubis The front part. The two pubic bones meet at the pubic symphysis.

The acetabulum: Where all three bones meet on the lateral side — the cup-shaped socket of the hip joint.
Memory trick: "I Sit on my Pubis" — Ilium (top), Ischium (bottom), Pubis (front). The acetabulum is where they all converge.
The femur — thigh bone Bone ▼
What it is: The longest, heaviest bone in the body. Runs from the hip to the knee.

Femoral head The ball of the hip joint — fits into the acetabulum.
Femoral neck Most common fracture site in elderly falls — "broken hip."
Greater trochanter Outer hip bump — key gluteal muscle attachment. Used as a landmark for hip injections.
Medial & lateral condyles Rounded bumps at the bottom forming the top of the knee joint.

Q angle: The angle the femur makes with the tibia. Wider pelvis → greater Q angle → more knee valgus → higher ACL risk. Women are anatomically at higher risk.
Clinical anchor: "Hip fracture = femoral neck until proven otherwise." The neck is thin and angled — high stress concentration.
Knee & lower leg — tibia, fibula, patella Bone ▼
Tibia (shin bone): The large weight-bearing bone. Its flat top (tibial plateau) forms the bottom of the knee. The tibial tuberosity on the front is where the patellar tendon attaches.

Fibula: The thin lateral bone — bears only ~15% of weight but critical for ankle stability. The lateral malleolus (outer ankle bump) is the bottom of the fibula.

Patella (kneecap): A sesamoid bone embedded in the quadriceps tendon. Acts as a pulley to improve quad mechanical advantage by ~50%. Glides in the femoral trochlear groove.
Memory trick: "Tibia = weight, Fibula = stability." Tibia is medial and massive; fibula is lateral and thin.
Foot & ankle bones Bone ▼
Tarsals (7) Back/mid foot. Talus sits on the calcaneus (heel). Ankle joint = talus + tibia + fibula.
Metatarsals (5) Mid-foot long bones. 5th metatarsal base = common fracture site in ankle sprains.
Phalanges (14) Toes — big toe has 2, others have 3.

Medial malleolus: Inner ankle bump — bottom of the tibia. Together with the lateral malleolus (fibula), forms the ankle mortise that grips the talus.
Memory trick: Back = tarsals, mid = metatarsals, front = phalanges. Same pattern as the hand (carpals, metacarpals, phalanges).
Key muscles
Gluteal muscles — the powerhouse Muscle ▼
Gluteus maximus: Largest muscle in the body. Primary hip extensor. Powers stair climbing, squatting, and running propulsion. Inferior gluteal nerve (L5–S2).

Gluteus medius: Side of the hip. Primary hip abductor and KEY pelvic stabilizer during walking. Weakness → Trendelenburg sign (pelvis drops on the opposite side). Superior gluteal nerve (L4–S1).

Gluteus minimus: Deep to the medius. Same actions — assists abduction and internal rotation.
Clinical anchor: Weak gluteus medius = Trendelenburg gait. You will assess this in virtually every lower-extremity patient you ever treat.
Quadriceps — front of the thigh Muscle ▼
Four muscles converging into the quad tendon → patella → patellar tendon → tibial tuberosity.

Rectus femoris Crosses hip AND knee — flexes hip, extends knee. Only quad that crosses the hip.
Vastus lateralis Outer thigh. Extends knee.
Vastus medialis Inner thigh. VMO portion critical for patellar tracking.
Vastus intermedius Deep under rectus. Extends knee.

All innervated by the femoral nerve (L2–L4). Patellar reflex (knee jerk) tests L4.
Memory trick: "Really Very Very Important" → Rectus femoris, Vastus lateralis, Vastus medialis, Vastus intermedius.
Hamstrings — back of the thigh Muscle ▼
Three muscles originating from the ischial tuberosity, all crossing hip and knee.

Biceps femoris Outer hamstring. Flexes knee, extends hip, externally rotates leg.
Semimembranosus Inner hamstring — flat. Flexes knee, extends hip, internally rotates.
Semitendinosus Inner hamstring — long tendon. Same as semimembranosus.

Innervation: Sciatic nerve (L5–S2). Most common muscle strain in sport — typically at the proximal ischial attachment. Straight leg raise test stretches both hamstrings and the sciatic nerve.
Memory trick: "2 semis on the inside, biceps on the outside." Biceps femoris is lateral; the two semi- muscles are medial.
Ankle & foot muscles Muscle ▼
Dorsiflexors (front of leg):
Tibialis anterior Main dorsiflexor + foot invertor. L4 myotome. Weakness = foot drop. Deep fibular nerve.

Plantarflexors (back of leg):
Gastrocnemius Two-headed calf. Plantarflexes ankle + assists knee flexion. S1–S2. Achilles reflex tests S1.
Soleus Deep to gastroc — only crosses ankle. Most important for push-off during walking.

Evertors:
Fibularis longus & brevis Evert the foot. Injured in lateral ankle sprains. Superficial fibular nerve (L5–S1).
Reflex anchors: Patellar = L4. Achilles = S1. These are the two lower extremity reflexes tested every clinical day.
Clinical connections
Gait basics — what muscles do what during walking Clinical ▼
Two phases: Stance (~60%) — foot on ground. Swing (~40%) — foot in air.

Key muscle roles by moment:
Initial contact (heel strike) → quads brake the knee; tibialis anterior decelerates foot slap.
Midstance (single leg) → gluteus medius stabilizes pelvis. This is the Trendelenburg moment.
Push-off (toe off) → gastrocnemius/soleus generate forward propulsion.
Swing → tibialis anterior lifts foot to clear the ground.
Clinical anchor: "Glutes stabilize, quads brake, soleus pushes, tib ant lifts." These four jobs cover 90% of gait analysis conversations.
Common lower extremity injuries Clinical ▼
ACL tear Anterior cruciate ligament — resists forward tibial sliding. Pivoting sports. Tested with Lachman test.

Lateral ankle sprain Most common musculoskeletal injury overall. Inversion → tears anterior talofibular ligament (ATFL) first.

Femoral neck fracture "Broken hip" in the elderly. High mortality. Osteoporosis is a major risk factor.

IT band syndrome Iliotibial band rubs over the lateral femoral epicondyle. Common in runners. Caused by weak hip abductors.
Diagnostic score: 50% — Priority focus area

Upper Extremity

Shoulder girdle bones, the rotator cuff, brachial plexus terminal branches, and common arm/hand injuries. Pay extra attention to the brachial plexus and SITS muscles.

Bones & landmarks
Shoulder girdle — clavicle & scapula Bone ▼
Clavicle (collarbone): The only bony connection between the arm and the axial skeleton. Acts as a strut. Most commonly fractured bone from a fall on an outstretched hand (FOOSH).

Scapula (shoulder blade): Flat triangular bone floating on the ribcage, held only by muscles.
Acromion Tip of the scapular spine — forms the roof of the shoulder. Supraspinatus passes beneath it. Narrowing → impingement.
Glenoid Shallow socket that receives the humeral head — the "cup" of the shoulder joint.
Coracoid process Beak-shaped projection on the front — attachment for several muscles and ligaments.
Memory trick: Clavicle comes from Latin clavis = key. It "unlocks" arm movement relative to the trunk.
Humerus, radius & ulna Bone ▼
Humerus (upper arm):
Surgical neck Most common fracture site. Axillary nerve wraps around here — fracture can damage it.
Greater & lesser tubercles Where rotator cuff muscles attach.
Lateral epicondyle Outer elbow — wrist extensor attachment. Overuse → "tennis elbow."
Medial epicondyle Inner elbow — wrist flexor attachment. Ulnar nerve passes behind it — "funny bone."

Radius: Thumb side of forearm. Distal radius fracture = Colles fracture from FOOSH.
Ulna: Pinky side. Has the olecranon = the point of the elbow.
Memory trick: "Radius = thumb side." R for Radius, R for Right-thumb side in anatomical position.
Hand & wrist bones Bone ▼
Carpals (8) Wrist bones in two rows. Scaphoid most commonly fractured — can lose blood supply if missed.
Metacarpals (5) Palm bones 1–5. Boxer's fracture = 5th metacarpal neck.
Phalanges (14) Thumb has 2; fingers have 3 each (proximal, middle, distal).

Carpal tunnel: U-shaped canal formed by carpal bones, bridged by the transverse carpal ligament. Contains 9 tendons + the median nerve. Compression → carpal tunnel syndrome (numbness in thumb, index, middle fingers).
Carpal bones: "Some Lovers Try Positions That They Can't Handle" → Scaphoid, Lunate, Triquetrum, Pisiform, Trapezium, Trapezoid, Capitate, Hamate.
Key muscles
Rotator cuff — the SITS muscles Muscle ▼
Group function: Compress the humeral head into the glenoid (stabilization) and rotate the arm. Without them the shoulder dislocates easily — the glenoid is like a golf ball on a tee.

Supraspinatus Above the scapular spine. Initiates first 15–30° of abduction. Most commonly torn. Suprascapular nerve (C5–C6).
Infraspinatus Below the scapular spine. External rotation. Suprascapular nerve (C5–C6).
Teres minor Beside infraspinatus. External rotation. Axillary nerve (C5–C6).
Subscapularis Front of scapula. Internal rotation. Subscapular nerves (C5–C7). The only one on the front.
Memory trick: "SITS" — Supraspinatus, Infraspinatus, Teres minor, Subscapularis. Subscapularis is the odd one out — it lives on the front of the scapula.
Deltoid & major shoulder movers Muscle ▼
Deltoid: Three parts — Anterior (flexion, internal rotation), Middle (abduction above 30°), Posterior (extension, external rotation). All innervated by the axillary nerve (C5–C6).

Pectoralis major: Large chest muscle. Adducts and internally rotates. Medial and lateral pectoral nerves (C5–T1).

Latissimus dorsi: The big "lats." Extends, adducts, internally rotates. Thoracodorsal nerve (C6–C8). Key for crutch walking and transfers.
Clinical anchor: Axillary nerve = deltoid + teres minor. It wraps around the surgical neck of the humerus — why shoulder dislocations must be assessed for nerve injury.
Biceps, triceps & forearm muscles Muscle ▼
Biceps brachii: Flexes elbow AND supinates (turns palm up). Musculocutaneous nerve (C5–C6). Biceps reflex = C5–C6.

Triceps brachii: The only elbow extensor. Radial nerve (C7). Triceps reflex = C7.

Forearm flexors (anterior): Flex wrist and fingers. Mostly median nerve + some ulnar.

Forearm extensors (posterior): All radial nerve. Radial nerve injury → wrist drop.
Reflex anchors: Biceps = C5. Brachioradialis = C6. Triceps = C7. Count up: 5, 6, 7.
Key concept
The brachial plexus — a beginner's map Concept ▼
What it is: The nerve network from C5–T1 supplying the entire arm. Spinal levels come in, rearrange, and five terminal nerves come out.

Musculocutaneous Biceps, coracobrachialis, lateral forearm sensation.
Axillary Deltoid, teres minor, lateral upper arm sensation.
Radial Triceps + all wrist/finger extensors. Injury = wrist drop.
Median Most forearm flexors + thenar hand muscles. Carpal tunnel = median nerve compression.
Ulnar Intrinsic hand muscles + ring/pinky sensation. Injury = claw hand.
Memory trick: "My Aunt Rides Many Unicycles" → Musculocutaneous, Axillary, Radial, Median, Ulnar.
Common upper extremity injuries Clinical ▼
Rotator cuff tear Supraspinatus most common. Painful arc 60–120°. PT is first-line before surgery.
Shoulder impingement Supraspinatus compressed under the acromion. Overhead activities. Weak rotator cuff + poor scapular control.
Tennis elbow Wrist extensor overuse at the lateral epicondyle. Pain with gripping.
Carpal tunnel syndrome Median nerve compression. Thumb/index/middle finger numbness. Worse at night. Phalen's and Tinel's tests.
FOOSH injuries Fall on outstretched hand → clavicle fracture, scaphoid fracture, Colles fracture. Know all three.
Clinical anchor: "FOOSH" — one of the most documented injury mechanisms in PT. Three main fractures: clavicle, scaphoid, distal radius.
Diagnostic score: 75% — Review and reinforce

Back & Neck

Vertebral regions, disc anatomy, spinal nerve roots, deep paraspinal muscles, and radiculopathy patterns. The myotome and disc cards are highest priority.

Spinal structure
The vertebral column — regions & numbers Bone ▼
Cervical (C1–C7) 7 vertebrae. The neck. Most mobile. C1 (atlas) holds the skull; C2 (axis) allows rotation.
Thoracic (T1–T12) 12 vertebrae. Mid-back. Ribs attach here. Limited motion due to rib cage.
Lumbar (L1–L5) 5 vertebrae. Lower back. Largest — bear most weight. Most common disc herniation site.
Sacral (S1–S5) 5 fused segments forming the sacrum. Connects spine to pelvis at the sacroiliac joints.
Coccygeal The tailbone. 3–4 fused segments. Can fracture from a fall onto the buttocks.

Total = 33 vertebrae; 26 moveable segments in adults (sacral and coccygeal are fused).
Memory trick: "Breakfast at 7, Lunch at 12, Dinner at 5" → Cervical=7, Thoracic=12, Lumbar=5.
Anatomy of a single vertebra Bone ▼
Vertebral body Drum-shaped front portion — the weight-bearing part. Stacked like coins, separated by discs.
Spinous process The bony spike you feel running down your back.
Transverse processes Two wings pointing sideways — muscle attachment and rib articulation in thoracic region.
Facet joints Small flat joints between adjacent vertebrae — orientation determines motion available at each level.
Pedicles Short pillars connecting arch to body. Between adjacent pedicles is the intervertebral foramen — where spinal nerves exit.
Clinical anchor: The intervertebral foramen is where disc herniations and bone spurs compress spinal nerves, causing the radiating pain PTs assess constantly.
Intervertebral discs Concept ▼
What they are: Cartilaginous shock absorbers between each vertebral body — about 25% of spinal height. You are literally taller in the morning (discs rehydrate overnight).

Nucleus pulposus Soft gel-like inner core. Absorbs compressive load. Like jelly donut filling.
Annulus fibrosus Tough outer ring of fibrous layers. Resists rotation and bending forces. Like the donut wall.

Disc herniation: Nucleus pushes through annulus crack → presses on nerve root → radiculopathy. Most common at L4–L5 and L5–S1 (lumbar), C5–C6 (cervical).
Memory trick: "Jelly donut" — nucleus = jelly, annulus = dough. Herniation = jelly squirting out when you squeeze the donut, pressing on the nearby nerve.
Nerve roots & myotomes
Spinal nerve roots & key myotomes Concept ▼
Each spinal level sends a nerve to a specific body region. Myotomes = muscles controlled by that level. Dermatomes = skin sensation supplied by that level.

Key myotomes:
C5Shoulder abduction (deltoid)
C6Elbow flexion (biceps), wrist extension
C7Elbow extension (triceps), wrist flexion
L4Knee extension (quads), ankle dorsiflexion — patellar reflex
L5Great toe extension — no reliable reflex
S1Ankle plantarflexion (calf) — Achilles reflex
Clinical anchor: "No L5 reflex" — L5 has no deep tendon reflex. Knowing this prevents misdiagnosing L5 involvement as a reflex-present finding.
Back muscles
Superficial back muscles — the movers Muscle ▼
Trapezius Diamond-shaped upper back + neck. Upper = elevates shoulder. Middle = retracts scapula. Lower = depresses scapula. CN XI + C3–C4.

Latissimus dorsi Lower back. Arm extension, adduction, internal rotation. Thoracodorsal nerve. Key for crutch walking.

Rhomboids Between scapula and spine. Retract and elevate the scapula. Dorsal scapular nerve (C4–C5). Weakness → rounded shoulders.

Serratus anterior Protracts and upwardly rotates scapula for overhead reaching. Long thoracic nerve (C5–C7). Weakness → winged scapula.
Clinical anchor: Weak lower trap + weak serratus anterior = most common cause of shoulder impingement. You'll treat this constantly.
Deep back muscles — the stabilizers Muscle ▼
All innervated by dorsal rami of spinal nerves.

Erector spinae The large column you feel on either side of the spine. Three groups (iliocostalis, longissimus, spinalis). Extends the spine and resists forward bending. Most worked muscle during lifting.

Multifidus Deep to erector spinae. Short segments spanning 2–4 vertebrae. Provides fine-tuned segmental stability rather than gross motion. Atrophies quickly with back pain — primary rehab target in low back pain.

Rotatores Deepest and shortest — span 1–2 levels. Function more as proprioceptors (position sensors) than movers.
Clinical anchor: Multifidus is arguably the most important muscle in all of musculoskeletal PT. Retraining it after a back injury is a cornerstone of low back pain rehab.
Clinical connections
Radiculopathy — pinched nerve basics Clinical ▼
What it means: Irritation or compression of a nerve root as it exits the spine — from a herniated disc, bone spur, or narrowed foramen.

Pattern of symptoms: Pain, numbness, or weakness that follows a specific dermatome/myotome pattern down the arm or leg. Not just local back/neck pain — symptoms travel.

Sciatica L4, L5, or S1 compression → pain running down the back of the leg. Positive straight leg raise test.
Cervical radiculopathy C5–C7 most common → pain/numbness into the arm. Positive Spurling's test.
Clinical anchor: "Pain that travels in a stripe down the limb = think nerve root. Pain that is local and diffuse = think muscle or joint." First sorting rule for back/neck pain.
Diagnostic score: 0% — Start here

Thorax & Abdomen

The ribcage, sternum, diaphragm, intercostals, abdominal wall, and cardiovascular basics. The diaphragm card is your single most important read in this section.

Bones & landmarks
The ribcage (thoracic cage) Bone ▼
Made of: 12 pairs of ribs + 12 thoracic vertebrae (T1–T12) + the sternum. Protects heart and lungs.

True ribs (1–7) Connect directly to the sternum via their own cartilage.
False ribs (8–10) Connect to the cartilage of rib 7, not directly to the sternum.
Floating ribs (11–12) Don't connect to the sternum at all. Most vulnerable to fracture.

PT relevance: Rib fractures are extremely painful because the intercostals are constantly moving with every breath. Even a small fracture can significantly limit breathing.
Memory trick: "7 True, 3 False, 2 Floating" — 7+3+2 = 12 pairs total.
The sternum Bone ▼
The flat breastbone running down the center of the chest. Three parts:

Manubrium Top — where the clavicles and first two ribs attach.
Body Middle — where ribs 3–7 attach.
Xiphoid process Bottom tip — CPR hand placement landmark and diaphragm attachment site.

Sternal angle (angle of Louis): The ridge where manubrium meets body. Marks where the trachea splits into two bronchi, and the level of T4/T5. One of the most important landmarks in the whole body.
Memory trick: "MBX" — Manubrium, Body, Xiphoid. Top to bottom.
Key muscles — breathing & trunk
The diaphragm — the #1 breathing muscle Muscle ▼
What it looks like: A dome-shaped muscle (like a parachute or upside-down bowl) separating the chest from the abdomen.

Attachments: Around the rim — xiphoid process, lower ribs 6–12, and lumbar vertebrae L1–L3 via the crura.

Breathing mechanics:
Inhale Diaphragm contracts → dome flattens downward → chest volume increases → pressure drops → air rushes IN.
Quiet exhale Diaphragm relaxes → dome rises → chest volume decreases → air flows OUT. Passive — no muscle effort needed.

Three openings (hiatus):
T8 Inferior vena cava passes through.
T10 Esophagus + vagus nerve. Site of hiatal hernias.
T12 Aorta passes through.
Nerve supply: Phrenic nerve (C3–C5).
Memory trick: "I 8 (T8) 10 eggs (T10 esophagus) At 12 (T12 aorta)." Also: "C3, 4, 5 keeps the diaphragm alive" — the phrenic nerve levels.
Intercostal muscles Muscle ▼
Three layers of muscle filling the spaces between each pair of ribs — like webbing between the ribs.

External intercostals Outermost. Fibers run downward-forward (hands in pockets). RAISE ribs during inhalation — assist the diaphragm.
Internal intercostals Middle layer. Fibers run perpendicular to external. LOWER ribs during forced exhalation (coughing, blowing).
Innermost intercostals Deepest. Similar to internal — also forced exhalation.

Clinical note: Rib fracture pain with every breath is caused by constant intercostal movement — why rib fractures are sometimes more limiting than they appear on imaging.
Memory trick: External = inhalation (E = air in). Internal = expIratIon (both have double "I" sounds).
Abdominal muscles — the four layers Muscle ▼
No bony protection in front, so four overlapping muscle layers in different directions create strength from every angle — like plywood.

Rectus abdominis The "six-pack" — runs vertically down the center. Flexes the trunk. Separated by the linea alba (fibrous midline seam).
External oblique Outermost diagonal. Fibers run downward-inward (hands in pockets). Rotates trunk to the OPPOSITE side.
Internal oblique Middle diagonal. Fibers run upward-inward. Rotates trunk to the SAME side.
Transverse abdominis (TrA) Deepest — fibers run horizontally like a corset. Does NOT produce movement. KEY muscle for spinal stabilization. Commonly weak in low back pain.
Memory trick: "REIT" — Rectus, External, Internal, Transverse. Superficial to deep.
Cardiovascular basics
The heart — four chambers, two circuits Concept ▼
Pulmonary circuit: Right side of heart → lungs → left side. Picks up O₂, drops off CO₂.
Systemic circuit: Left side → entire body → right side. Delivers O₂ to all tissues.

The path of blood (memorize this):
Body → Superior/Inferior Vena Cava → Right Atrium → (tricuspid valve) → Right Ventricle → (pulmonary valve) → Pulmonary Artery → Lungs → Pulmonary Veins → Left Atrium → (mitral valve) → Left Ventricle → (aortic valve) → Aorta → Body

Classic exam trap: The pulmonary ARTERY carries DEoxygenated blood. The pulmonary VEINS carry oxygenated blood. Opposite of every other vessel in the body.
Memory trick: "Arteries Away from the heart, Veins return" — true for pulmonary vessels too. The oxygen status is just counterintuitive.
Major vessels of the thorax Concept ▼
Aortic arch branches (3, in order):
Brachiocephalic trunk First — splits into right subclavian (arm) and right common carotid (head/neck).
Left common carotid Second — left side of neck and head.
Left subclavian Third — left arm.

The venae cavae (return blood to right atrium):
Superior vena cava (SVC) Drains everything above the diaphragm — head, neck, arms.
Inferior vena cava (IVC) Drains everything below — abdomen, pelvis, legs.
Aortic arch branches: "Big Leagues, Carry Score" → Brachiocephalic, Left Carotid, Left Subclavian.
Clinical connections
Breathing mechanics — what changes with injury Clinical ▼
Quiet breathing: Almost entirely the diaphragm. Chest barely moves.
Deep breathing/exercise: Diaphragm + external intercostals + scalenes + sternocleidomastoid (neck muscles kick in).
Forced exhalation (coughing/sneezing): Internal intercostals + abdominals squeeze to force air out fast.

Diaphragm paralysis (e.g. high cervical SCI at C3–C5): primary breathing muscle lost → ventilator required.

Diaphragmatic breathing in PT: Teaching patients to breathe "into the belly" is one of the most common interventions in pulmonary rehab and pain management.
Clinical anchor: "C3, 4, 5 keeps the diaphragm alive." Phrenic nerve = C3–C5.
Referred pain — why heart attacks feel like arm pain Clinical ▼
Referred pain: Pain felt in a location that is NOT where the actual problem is. The brain gets confused because nerves from different regions share the same spinal pathways.

Heart → Left arm/jaw: The heart shares nerve pathways with the left arm (T1–T4 dermatomes). Heart muscle pain refers to the arm or jaw, not just the chest.

Diaphragm → Shoulder tip: Phrenic nerve (C3–C5). Diaphragm irritation (blood, infection) refers pain to the shoulder tip — called Kehr's sign. Can indicate ruptured spleen or liver injury.

Why this matters in PT: Patients who come in for "shoulder pain" may have thoracic or visceral causes. Learning to screen for red flags is a core DPT competency.
Diagnostic score: 75% — Review and reinforce

Clinical Reasoning

How PTs think: the 5-question framework, dermatomes, deep tendon reflexes, classic nerve injury patterns, and practice vignettes with hidden answers.

The framework
How PTs think — the 5-question framework Framework ▼
Every patient presentation, run these five questions in order:

1. What region? Spine, joint, muscle, nerve — where in the body?
2. What structure? Bone, disc, ligament, tendon, nerve root, peripheral nerve, muscle?
3. What level? Which spinal level (C5? L4? S1)? Use myotomes and dermatomes to localize.
4. What deficit? What function is lost? Strength, sensation, reflex, range of motion?
5. What mechanism? How did it happen? Trauma, overuse, postural, disease?

Every clinical vignette in your career is just filling in these five blanks.
Anchor phrase: "Region → Structure → Level → Deficit → Mechanism." Say it until it's automatic.
Dermatomes — pain maps of the body Concept ▼
A dermatome is a strip of skin supplied by a single spinal nerve root. Nerve root compression → sensory changes (numbness, tingling, burning) follow that dermatome pattern.

Key dermatomes to know:
C6Thumb and index finger
C7Middle finger
C8Ring and pinky finger
L4Medial lower leg and big toe (inner)
L5Dorsum (top) of the foot
S1Lateral foot and sole
Memory trick: "C6 = thumb (6 looks like a thumb). C7 = middle (tallest finger = tallest cervical). C8 = pinky (last finger, last cervical)."
Deep tendon reflexes — your neurological speedometer Concept ▼
Reflexes test the integrity of the reflex arc at a specific spinal level. Fast, objective, requires no patient cooperation.

Biceps reflex Tap biceps tendon → elbow flexes. Tests C5–C6.
Brachioradialis reflex Tap brachioradialis → forearm flexes. Tests C6.
Triceps reflex Tap triceps tendon → elbow extends. Tests C7.
Patellar reflex Tap patellar tendon → knee extends. Tests L4.
Achilles reflex Tap Achilles tendon → foot plantarflexes. Tests S1.
L5 — no reflex L5 has no reliable deep tendon reflex. High-yield clinical fact.

Interpreting: Diminished = lower motor neuron/nerve root. Exaggerated = upper motor neuron (brain/cord). Absent = significant nerve damage.
Reflex levels: "5, 6, 7 up top (biceps, brachioradialis, triceps) — 4, nothing, 1 down below (patellar, L5 has none, Achilles)."
Classic nerve injury patterns
The five headline nerve injury presentations Clinical ▼
Wrist drop Radial nerve (C7). Cannot extend the wrist. Compressed in the radial groove ("Saturday night palsy").

Foot drop Common fibular nerve or L4/L5 root. Cannot dorsiflect the foot. Causes steppage gait (high stepping to clear the toes).

Claw hand Ulnar nerve. Ring and pinky fingers claw. Loss of intrinsic hand muscles.

Trendelenburg gait Superior gluteal nerve / gluteus medius weakness. Pelvis drops to the opposite side during single-leg stance.

Winged scapula Long thoracic nerve / serratus anterior weakness. Scapula wings away from the ribcage when pushing against a wall.
Pattern anchor: "Wrist drop = radial. Foot drop = fibular/L4–L5. Claw = ulnar. Winging = long thoracic. Trendelenburg = superior gluteal."
Practice vignettes
Vignette 1 — the construction worker Clinical ▼
Patient presentation
A 45-year-old construction worker reports that after carrying heavy loads on his shoulder all day, his right arm feels weak. He cannot raise his arm past 20° and the outside of his upper arm is numb. His deltoid appears smaller than the left side.
Questions: Which nerve is likely injured? Which muscle is affected? Why is the upper arm numb?
Axillary nerve (C5–C6) injury — compressed at the surgical neck of the humerus from the heavy load. The axillary nerve innervates the deltoid (arm abduction) and teres minor, and provides sensation to the lateral upper arm ("regimental badge" area). Deltoid atrophy develops with chronic injury.
Vignette 2 — the runner with knee pain Clinical ▼
Patient presentation
A 28-year-old marathon runner has lateral knee pain starting at mile 8 of long runs. Sharp, worse going downstairs. Her right hip "dips" when she stands on that leg. No trauma, no swelling.
Questions: What structure is causing the lateral knee pain? What does the hip dip indicate?
Two issues: (1) IT band syndrome — the iliotibial band rubbing over the lateral femoral epicondyle. (2) The hip dip is a Trendelenburg sign indicating gluteus medius weakness. Weak hip abductors increase valgus knee stress and are a common underlying cause of IT band syndrome. Treatment targets both the local knee AND the proximal hip.
Vignette 3 — the office worker with hand numbness Clinical ▼
Patient presentation
A 38-year-old accountant reports her thumb, index, and middle finger go numb at night. She sometimes drops her coffee cup in the morning. She types 8–10 hours/day. Shaking her hands relieves the numbness temporarily.
Questions: Which nerve is compressed? Where? What structure is causing it?
Median nerve compression in the carpal tunnel — carpal tunnel syndrome. Nighttime symptoms occur because the wrist drops into flexion during sleep, narrowing the tunnel. Morning weakness reflects thenar muscle (thumb grip) involvement. The transverse carpal ligament forms the "roof" of the tunnel and is released in carpal tunnel surgery.
Red flags
Red flags — when anatomy signals danger Clinical ▼
Cauda equina syndrome Saddle anesthesia (groin/inner thigh numbness) + bowel/bladder loss + bilateral leg weakness. EMERGENCY — immediate surgical referral. The most critical red flag in all of PT.

Bilateral symptoms Both arms or both legs from the same spinal complaint = spinal cord involvement (myelopathy). Urgent.

Night pain + weight loss Back pain waking you from sleep + unexplained weight loss = potential malignancy. PTs screen for cancer.

Upper motor neuron signs Hyperreflexia, clonus, Babinski sign (big toe extends when you stroke the sole) = brain or spinal cord pathology, not peripheral nerve.
Non-negotiable anchor: "Saddle anesthesia + bowel/bladder = cauda equina = refer immediately." Memorize cold before day one of lab.
